Results

Overview

With the aim to create a lipid-producing cell factory, we desgined a set of strains with modifications to improve various aspects lipid metabolism. We show that decreasing TAG turnover by deletion of TAG lipases results in increased lipid levels. Light-activated expression of perilipin promotes lipid build-up, presumably by sequestering TAG to lipid droplets. Finally, we show that indium phosphide nanoparticles can functionalize yeast cells to use light energy for lipid synthesis.

TAG lipase deletions lead to significantly increased lipid accumulation

First, we studied the effects of different TAG lipase (TGL3, TGL4, TGL6) and ZWF1 gene deletions on lipid production in a batch fermentation experiment. The cultures were set to grow at equal densities and at 24 and 72 hours after the initial dilution, the lipid accumulation was monitored by Nile red staining. Nile red is a fluorescent stain that interacts specifically with lipids in lipid droplets (LDs) and it does not stain the cellular membranes (Greenspan et al., 1985) . At 24 hours after dilution, the cells from wild-type strain showed very low Nile red staining, indicative of low intracellular lipid levels (Fig 1, 2A). Single deletions of TGL3 and TGL4 resulted in a considerable increase in lipid accumulation, with tgl3Δ having a slightly greater effect than tgl4Δ (Fig 1, 2A). Interestingly, in the cells with TAG lipase deletions, clearly identifiable LDs could be detected in both the bright-field image and the Nile red fluorescent image (Fig 1). Although tgl3Δ tgl4Δ double deletion did not increase the lipid levels in LDs further, a triple deletion of all major TAG lipases (tgl3Δ tgl4Δ tgl5Δ) had a profound positive effect on lipid build-up. In the 24-hour time point, the Nile red staining intensity of the triple TAG lipase deletion strain was not affected by deletion of ZWF1 (Fig 1, 2A).

The lipid accumulation was also measured at 72 hours following dilution. In this time point, the culture had reached the stationary phase, and LDs were clearly detectable with Nile red staining for all tested strains, including the wild-type (Fig 2B). Lipid accumulation is known to increase in S. cerevisiae cells upon nutrient depletion (Werner-Washburne et al., 1993), and our results reveal that the difference between wild-type and TAG lipase deletion strains is considerably reduced in the stationary phase cells (Fig 2B). In the 72-hour time point, we did not observe a statistically significant difference between wild-type and the single tgl4Δ or double tgl3Δ tgl4Δ deletion cells. Single deletion of TGL3, which had the largest effect in the 24-hour time point, also led to increased LD staining intensity at 72 hours. We observed the highest intracellular lipid levels with the triple tgl3Δ tgl4Δ tgl5Δ deletion strain, and interestingly, in this time point, the zwf1Δ strain had considerably lower LD staining, comparable to the level of the wild-type strain (Fig 2B). Although at the lipid levels of zwf1Δ strain were not lower in the 24-hour time point, ZWF1 deletion could be expected to result in decreased lipid synthesis, as Zwf1 is required to regenerate NADPH, a critical cofactor in fatty acid synthesis. Taken together, by preventing TAG degradation with triple deletion of TAG lipases, we have achieved a considerable increase in lipid production compared to the wild-type strain.

Expression of human perilipin Plin3 results in higher intracellular lipid levels

As our next step to improve lipid bioproduction, we tested whether expression of human perilipin Plin3 could further increase the lipid bioproduction in the tgl3Δ tgl4Δ tgl5Δ zwf1Δ strain. For this, we constructed and transformed to these cells a plasmid that contains the light-inducible transcription factor VP-EL222 expression cassette and the PLIN3 expression cassette with the light-regulated promoter. The cells containing this plasmid were set to grow at 30 °C either under constant illumination or in the dark. In 24 hours, the lipid accumulation in LDs was monitored by Nile red staining. Interestingly, we detected significantly higher lipid levels in the cells that were grown under illumination, which activates PLIN3 expression via VP-EL222 (Fig 4). As Plin3 is expected to promote LD assembly, the observed effect could be due to stabilization of the synthesized TAGs. We did not observe any noticable changes in LD morphology between cells that either express or do not express Plin3 (Fig 4A). These results show that the lipid bioproduction in S. cerevisiae could be further improved by expression of human perilipin Plin3.

But why use non-oleaginous S.cerevisae instead of oleaginous yeast in the first place?

Although different oleaginous yeasts have been engineered to produce neutral lipids, such as TAGs in high levels, these lipid producing yeasts need high cell density fermentation for intracellular accumulation. Additionally, it may be challenging to recover the products from the cells. S. cerevisiae, on the other hand, is a robust system tolerating extreme fermentation conditions (Zhou et al., 2016) . Moreover, S. cerevisiae is a very well studied organism and is genetically tractable (Ma et al., 2018) .

Push, Pull and Protect

We have taken our inspiration from a strategy that has successfully worked in plants: “Push, Pull and Protect”. It involves optimizing the flux of carbon into TAG (storage form of lipids) by engineering of lipid-synthesizing enzymes to increase the fatty acid synthesis (Push), increasing TAG assembly (Pull) and downregulating lipid turnover (Protect). (Vanhercke et al., 2014)

Fatty acid biosynthesis takes place mainly in the cytosol in a series of reactions that convert the biosynthetic precursor acetyl‐CoA to long chain fatty acids (i.e. fatty acids that contain more than 14 carbon atoms). The first step of the conversion of acetyl‐CoA to fatty acids is its carboxylation to malonyl‐CoA, a reaction catalyzed by the ACC1‐encoded acetyl‐CoA carboxylase (YALI0C11407g). (Fakas, 2017) It is a well-known rate-limiting step. (Sheng & Feng, 2015)

In S. cerevisiae, the activity of Acc1 is post-translationally regulated by Snf1 protein kinase. Introduction of three mutations in Acc1 phosphorylation sites S686A, S659A, and S1157A has been found to lead to an enhanced Acc1 activity which causes an increase in availability of malonyl-CoA together with its products (Chen et al., 2018) . How this relates to our project … Fatty acyl-CoAs generated by fatty acid synthase (FAS) from malonyl-CoA enter the pathway of the major storage lipid TAG.
