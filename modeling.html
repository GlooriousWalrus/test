Lipids

Lipids are essential units and building-blocks of cellular membranes with several other biological roles within the cell. They serve the cell as energy storehouses and they also act as significant signaling molecules (Olzmann & Carvalho, 2019).

Neutral lipids are known as a subgroup of lipids consisting of hydrophobic molecules lacking charged groups. Triacylglycerols (TAGs) and sterol esters (SE) represent the major part of neutral lipids that are stored in lipid droplets (LDs). LDs are highly influential and dynamic storage organelles emerging from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), an organelle with which lipid droplets hold a connection during their life cycle. LDs are made of a hydrophobic core which is comprised of neutral lipids. The inner center of this hydrophobic core consists of triacylglycerols (TAG) which is enclosed by several layers of sterol esters. The core is then, surrounded and encompassed by a final phospholipid monolayer which is covered by perilipin proteins that affect the regulation of lipid metabolism. LDs have membrane contact sites with other cellular organelles, such as Golgi or mitochondria, facilitating the exchange of lipids, ions and metabolites. (Athenstaedt, 2010)

S.cerevisae

For LD production, we decided to use Saccharomyces cerevisiae as we thought it is a well experimented organism. Unlike other species of yeast, Saccharomyces cerevisiae has a low potential for producing lipids (Johnson et al., 1972) . TAG content makes up around 1% of the cell’s dry weight, therefore genes involved in the metabolic pathway of lipid production can be manipulated to enhance the level of TAG in yeast (Ferreira et al., 2018) . Which can result in the accumulation of neutral lipid into lipid droplets.

But why use non-oleaginous S.cerevisae instead of oleaginous yeast in the first place?

Although different oleaginous yeasts have been engineered to produce neutral lipids, such as TAGs in high levels, these lipid producing yeasts need high cell density fermentation for intracellular accumulation. Additionally, it may be challenging to recover the products from the cells. S. cerevisiae, on the other hand, is a robust system tolerating extreme fermentation conditions (Zhou et al., 2016) . Moreover, S. cerevisiae is a very well studied organism and is genetically tractable (Ma et al., 2018) .

Push, Pull and Protect

We have taken our inspiration from a strategy that has successfully worked in plants: “Push, Pull and Protect”. It involves optimizing the flux of carbon into TAG (storage form of lipids) by engineering of lipid-synthesizing enzymes to increase the fatty acid synthesis (Push), increasing TAG assembly (Pull) and downregulating lipid turnover (Protect). (Vanhercke et al., 2014)

Fatty acid biosynthesis takes place mainly in the cytosol in a series of reactions that convert the biosynthetic precursor acetyl‐CoA to long chain fatty acids (i.e. fatty acids that contain more than 14 carbon atoms). The first step of the conversion of acetyl‐CoA to fatty acids is its carboxylation to malonyl‐CoA, a reaction catalyzed by the ACC1‐encoded acetyl‐CoA carboxylase (YALI0C11407g). (Fakas, 2017) It is a well-known rate-limiting step. (Sheng & Feng, 2015)

In S. cerevisiae, the activity of Acc1 is post-translationally regulated by Snf1 protein kinase. Introduction of three mutations in Acc1 phosphorylation sites S686A, S659A, and S1157A has been found to lead to an enhanced Acc1 activity which causes an increase in availability of malonyl-CoA together with its products (Chen et al., 2018) . How this relates to our project … Fatty acyl-CoAs generated by fatty acid synthase (FAS) from malonyl-CoA enter the pathway of the major storage lipid TAG.
